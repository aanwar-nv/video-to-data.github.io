Overview

Human demonstrations are becoming a central source of data for robot learning. They offer a path toward robots that learn from rich, diverse human experience rather than only from robot-specific datasets. Yet observing humans is not the same as learning robot behavior.

Human demonstrations now come from internet videos, egocentric recordings, motion capture, teleoperation, wearable devices, kinesthetic teaching, VR/AR interfaces, simulation retargeting, and real-to-sim-to-real pipelines. These sources vary in embodiment, viewpoint, observability, physical fidelity, action labels, contact information, task structure, and scale. The field still lacks agreement on when such data is useful, what should be extracted from it, and how its value should be measured. This workshop asks one focused question:

How can robots learn reliably from human demonstrations?

This question cuts across direct video-to-policy learning, goal and reward inference, affordance learning, world modeling, real2sim2real reconstruction, simulation grounding, embodied foundation models, and more. Across these approaches, the central challenge is the same: human demonstrations are not automatically robot data. Robots need some form of conversion, grounding, or evaluation before human data becomes useful learning signal.

This workshop provides a focused venue to define the bottlenecks, compare assumptions, and produce shared evaluation criteria and near-term research directions. The workshop is designed as a working session, not a sequence of talks — built around a shared Video-to-Data Challenge, interactive failure-board and breakout sessions, and a collaboratively written post-workshop whitepaper.

The Video-to-Data Challenge

We instantiate the broad question of learning from human demos through the Video-to-Data Challenge and a corresponding baseline. To enable a variety of approaches, our baseline provides an end-to-end pipeline for ingesting long human videos, providing scene and hand reconstruction, retargeting hands to robot-compatible schemas, and training and evaluating policies in Isaac Lab. This baseline is intended to enable the community to investigate a variety of research questions relating to learning from human videos.

In addition to talks, the challenge enables participants to compare assumptions, metrics, and failure modes in a common setting. Winning teams will present concise walkthroughs of what worked, what failed, and what the next challenge should measure.

Research Questions

The workshop targets bottlenecks that prevent human demonstrations from becoming useful robot learning signal. Some examples:

  • Demonstration usefulness: what properties of human demonstrations predict downstream robot improvement, and when does more data help?
  • Representations for learning: should methods extract rewards, task graphs, scene models, robot motions, simulated episodes, or policies directly?
  • Grounding and control: when are reconstruction, simulation, real2sim2real, or world modeling necessary for feasible robot behavior?
  • Intent and embodiment: how can robots infer goals, constraints, contacts, affordances, and object states from humans with different bodies?
  • Evaluation and standards: what benchmarks, metrics, schemas, and failure taxonomies let the field compare approaches on common ground, especially as data sources vary (e.g., UMI-style data, human video)?

Call for Contributions

We solicit contributions across the following formats:

  • Full papers (4–8 pages): preliminary results, datasets, benchmark proposals, and negative results. Unlimited pages for references and appendices.
  • Position papers & extended abstracts (2 pages): to facilitate discussion and surface new opinions in the field.

We encourage work that is new, preliminary, or in progress. We discourage papers already accepted to the main CoRL 2026 program unless the submission is clearly reframed as a workshop position, dataset, benchmark, challenge submission, or failure analysis.
